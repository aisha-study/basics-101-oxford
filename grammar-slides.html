Unit 1C
One Word, Different Classes

Some words can belong to different classes depending on how they are used in a sentence. Always look at the role a word plays, not just the word itself.

As a VERB
Can I look at your photos?
We work on Saturday morning.
She prays five times a day.
As a NOUN
I like the look of that coat.
I'll be at work tomorrow.
Her daily prayer gives her peace.
🌙 Tip

Ask yourself: what is this word doing in the sentence? Is it naming something (noun), describing an action (verb), or describing another word (adjective / adverb)?

Subject, Verb, Object, Complement, and Adverbial — the five parts that build every English sentence.
Unit 2A
Five Main Sentence Structures

Every English sentence has a subject and a verb. We can then add more parts to it. There are 5 main structures.

1. Subject + Verb
Subject + Verb
My arms are aching. · Something happened.
2. Subject + Verb + Object
Subject + Verb + Object
I need a rest. · She reads the Quran.
3. Subject + Verb + Complement
Subject + Verb + Complement
This piano is heavy. · She is kind. (adj or noun phrase)
4. Subject + Verb + Adverbial
Subject + Verb + Adverbial
It is on my foot. · Iftar is after Maghrib.
5. Subject + Verb + Object + Object
Subject + Verb + Indirect Obj + Direct Obj
David bought Melanie a present. · She gave her sister a gift.
Oxford Practice Grammar — Unit 2A
✦
Unit 2B
Adverbials

We can add adverbials to all five sentence structures. An adverbial can be a prepositional phrase (e.g. on my foot) or an adverb (e.g. nearby).

  • 1
    My arms are aching terribly. (adverb)
  • 2
    I really need a rest. (adverb before verb)
  • 3
    Of course this piano is heavy. (phrase at start)
  • 4
    Fortunately, their house is nearby. (adverb at start)
  • 5
    David bought Melanie a present yesterday. (adverb at end)
🌙 Islamic Examples

She prays quietly in her room. · Alhamdulillah, the class went well. · We break our fast after Maghrib.

Unit 3A
Two Objects after a Verb

Some verbs (give, send, buy, tell, show...) can take two objects: an indirect object (the person receiving) and a direct object (the thing given).

Indirect Object firstDirect Object first (use to / for)
Henry gave Claire some flowers. Henry gave some flowers to Claire.
Emma gave Rachel a CD. Emma gave the CD to Rachel.
I'll send my cousin a postcard. I'll send a postcard to my cousin.
She gave her sister a gift. She gave a gift to her sister.

TO verbs: give, send, show, tell, lend, offer, teach, write, pass, pay, bring, read

FOR verbs: buy, make, book, cook, find, get, save, order, fetch, choose, build

Nouns
Types of Nouns

A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea. It is usually the subject or object of a sentence.

  • ✦
    Common Noun
    General names — city, book, sister, teacher
  • ✦
    Proper Noun
    Specific names — Maryam, Makkah, Ramadan
  • ✦
    Concrete Noun
    Things you can touch — Quran, hijab, masjid
  • ✦
    Abstract Noun
    Ideas & feelings — patience (sabr), love, faith

Verbs · Unit 4
The Present Continuous

The present continuous means we are in the middle of an action right now. Form: am/is/are + verb-ing

Positive
I am looking
She is reading
They are praying
Negative
I'm not looking
She isn't reading
They aren't praying
Question
Am I looking?
Is she reading?
Are they praying?
She is reciting the Quran right now.
We are learning English to better understand Islamic texts.

When to use it: Something happening NOW · something temporary · something changing over time (e.g. My English is getting better!)

Verbs · Unit 5
The Present Simple

The present simple is used for: thoughts & feelings, habits & routines, facts & permanent truths. Form: base verb (+s/es for he/she/it)

SubjectPositiveNegativeQuestion
I / you / we / they pray don't pray Do you pray?
he / she / it prays doesn't pray Does she pray?
Use it for:
  • ✦
    Thoughts & feelings: I believe, I love
  • ✦
    Habits: We pray five times a day
  • ✦
    Facts: The sun rises in the east
🌙 Islamic Examples

She reads the Quran every morning.
We pray five times a day.
Muslims fast during Ramadan.

Adjectives
Describing Nouns

An adjective describes or modifies a noun. It tells us about size, colour, feeling, number, opinion, and more. Adjectives usually come before the noun or after linking verbs (be, seem, look, feel).

Before a noun
a kind sister
a beautiful recitation
the white hijab
an important lesson
The generous sister gave food to the poor.
After a linking verb
The class is wonderful.
Her recitation sounds beautiful.
The students feel confident.
This exercise seems easy.
🌙 Islamic Examples

Ramadan is a blessed month.
She is patient and grateful.
The masjid looks peaceful.

Adverbs
Words that describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs — how, when, where, how often.
Adverbs
Types of Adverbs

An adverb modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb. Many adverbs are formed by adding -ly to an adjective.

TypeQuestions it answersExamples
MannerHow?quietly, slowly, carefully, loudly
TimeWhen?today, now, yesterday, soon
FrequencyHow often?always, never, often, daily
PlaceWhere?here, there, nearby, outside
DegreeHow much?very, quite, rather, too, enough
🌙 Islamic Examples

She quietly recited the Quran. · We always greet with Salam. · He prays five times daily. · She speaks very kindly to others.

Adjective → Adverb: quiet → quietly · beautiful → beautifully · patient → patiently

Prepositions
Words that show the relationship between a noun and another word — place, time, direction.
Prepositions
Prepositions of Place & Time

A preposition shows the relationship between a noun/pronoun and another word. Common prepositions: in, on, at, under, beside, between, before, after, behind, above

Prepositions of Place
The prayer mat is on the floor.
She sat beside her sister.
The Quran is in the bag.
The masjid is between the two shops.
Prepositions of Time
Iftar is after Maghrib.
Suhoor is before Fajr.
Class starts at 8 o'clock.
We fast in Ramadan.
🌙 Quick Tip

in — inside spaces, months, years · on — surfaces, days · at — specific points, times · before / after — sequence of events

A pronoun replaces a noun so we don't repeat it. We use subject pronouns before the verb and object pronouns after the verb.

Conjunctions
Connecting Ideas — FANBOYS

A conjunction joins words, phrases, or sentences. The most common are the FANBOYS: coordinating conjunctions.

LetterConjunctionUseIslamic Example
FForreasonShe rested, for she was tired after fasting.
AAndadditionI read Quran and made du'a.
NNornegative additionShe doesn't miss Fajr, nor does she miss Isha.
BButcontrastShe wanted to fast, but she was sick.
OOrchoiceYou can read Quran or make dhikr.
YYetcontrast (formal)It was difficult, yet she never gave up.
SSoresultIt was Ramadan, so we fasted.

Interjections
Expressing Emotion

An interjection is a word or phrase that expresses a sudden feeling or emotion. It is usually followed by ! and stands alone or at the start of a sentence.

General Interjections
  • !
    Wow! — surprise or amazement
  • !
    Oops! — small mistake
  • !
    Oh! — realization or surprise
  • !
    Ouch! — pain
  • !
    Hmm… — thinking or hesitation

  • ✦

  • ✦
    Astaghfirullah! — seeking forgiveness
  • ✦
    SubhanAllah! — wonder & praise
  • ✦
    InshaAllah! — if Allah wills
